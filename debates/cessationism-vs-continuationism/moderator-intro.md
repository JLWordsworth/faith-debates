# Moderator Introduction: Cessationism vs. Continuationism

## Welcome to Today's Debate

Welcome to this structured dialogue on one of the most significant and frequently debated topics within contemporary evangelical theology: **the continuation or cessation of the charismatic gifts**.

---

## Topic Overview

### What is Cessationism?

Cessationism is the theological position that certain spiritual gifts—particularly those considered "sign gifts" such as tongues, prophecy, and miraculous healing—were temporary in nature. Cessationists believe these gifts were given to the apostolic church for the purpose of confirming the authenticity of the apostles' message and the foundation of the New Testament. According to this view, once the apostolic era concluded and the New Testament canon was complete, these particular gifts ceased to operate.

### What is Continuationism?

Continuationism is the theological position that all spiritual gifts mentioned in the New Testament continue to be available and operative in the church today. Continuationists believe that the gifts of the Spirit—including tongues, prophecy, healing, and other charismatic gifts—remain active until the return of Christ, and that the Holy Spirit continues to distribute these gifts according to His will for the edification of the church and the advancement of the gospel.

### The Core Question

At the heart of this debate lies a fundamental question: **Did God intend for certain spiritual gifts to be temporary, confined to the apostolic era, or do all the gifts of the Spirit continue to be available to the church until Christ's return?**

---

## Why This Matters Today

This debate is not merely an academic exercise for theologians. It has profound implications for the life and practice of the church:

1. **Corporate Worship**: How should we expect the Holy Spirit to manifest in our gatherings? Should services include expressions of tongues, prophecy, or prayers for miraculous healing?

2. **Personal Spirituality**: How do individual believers understand and pursue the Spirit's work in their lives? What should they expect from their relationship with the Holy Spirit?

3. **Church Governance**: How do churches handle claims of prophetic utterance or other charismatic expressions? What authority structures are appropriate?

4. **Mission and Evangelism**: What role, if any, do signs and wonders play in contemporary gospel proclamation?

5. **Unity in the Body**: Christians across the spectrum genuinely desire to honor God and His Word. How can we maintain fellowship despite our differences on this issue?

Both cessationists and continuationists affirm the authority of Scripture, the necessity of the new birth, and the ongoing work of the Holy Spirit in the life of the believer. The disagreement lies in the scope and manner of the Spirit's work in this age.

---

## Ground Rules for Charitable Discourse

To ensure a productive and edifying exchange, both debaters agree to the following guidelines:

### 1. Represent Opposing Views Fairly

Each participant commits to accurately representing the other's position. The principle of charity requires engaging with the strongest form of the opposing view, not caricatures or straw men.

### 2. Maintain a Tone of Mutual Respect

Both debaters are brothers and sisters in Christ, united by far more than what divides them. Disagreement should never be personal, impugning motives, or questioning the other's commitment to Scripture or the gospel.

### 3. Prioritize Scripture

Both positions claim biblical warrant. The debate should center on textual exegesis, biblical theology, and sound hermeneutics—not anecdotes, testimonies, or appeals to experience alone (though experience may be referenced as relevant context).

### 4. Avoid Logical Fallacies

Arguments should be presented clearly, avoiding:
- Genetic fallacies (dismissing a view because of its associations)
- Proof-texting (citing verses without context)
- Ad hominem arguments
- Guilt by association

### 5. Acknowledge Complexity

This issue has been earnestly debated by godly Christians for centuries. Both debaters should acknowledge the strengths of genuine arguments on the other side.

### 6. Pursue Truth and Edification

The goal is not to "win" but to discover truth, sharpen understanding, and build up the body of Christ.

## Debate Structure

Our debate will proceed through the following phases:

### Phase 1: Opening Statements (3-5 paragraphs each)

Each debater will present their case, including:
- A clear statement of their position
- The primary biblical and theological foundations for their view
- A preview of key arguments to be developed

### Phase 2: First Rebuttals (4-6 paragraphs each)

Each debater will respond to the opponent's opening statement, addressing:
- Points of agreement where possible
- Specific disagreements and concerns
- Initial challenges to the opposing case

### Phase 3: Second Rebuttals (3-5 paragraphs each, optional)

Each debater may further develop their case and respond to the first rebuttal, including:
- Clarification of their position
- Additional supporting arguments
- Responses to critiques raised

### Phase 4: Cross-Examination (3 questions per side)

Each debater will pose three direct questions to the other, designed to:
- Clarify the opponent's position
- Challenge perceived weaknesses
- Explore implications of the opposing view

### Phase 5: Closing Statements (3-5 paragraphs each)

Each debater will summarize their case, including:
- The strongest arguments for their position
- Why their view best accounts for the biblical evidence
- Concluding thoughts for the audience's consideration
